Overview

Human-targeted cyber threats exploit human vulnerabilities to bypass security defenses, rather than attacking technology directly. For example, attackers use social engineering to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information—making technical defenses alone insufficient. Advances in AI have further amplified these risks by enabling attackers to generate highly convincing content at scale, making such threats increasingly difficult to detect by both human users and advanced security systems. Human-Targeted Cyber Threats and Defenses (HTCTD) focuses on threats designed to exploit human vulnerabilities, their countermeasures, and the evaluation of both risks and defenses from the human perspective. We bring together researchers, practitioners, and the public to explore these threats and countermeasures, raising awareness of their growing impact in today's rapidly evolving digital landscape.

Topics of Interest

We welcome submissions that explore the influence of human vulnerabilities in cybersecurity, such as security risks and defense strategies, system design, organizational practices, governance and policy, as well as works that evaluate/mitigate security threats through a human perspective. Topics of interest include (but are not limited to):
  • Human evaluation/awareness of cyber risks
  • AI-driven human-targeted attacks and mitigation
  • Phishing attacks and mitigation
  • Analysis of phishkit ecosystems
  • Online fraud
  • Fake news and misinformation on social media
  • Fake accounts/fake profiles on social network
  • Human-targeted attacks and defense
  • Cybersecurity education/training
  • Security and privacy considerations in human-centric systems
  • Human factors in authentication systems
  • Human-targeted malware
  • Governance, policy, and ethical challenges of human-centric security
  • Measurement studies
  • Systematization of knowledge (SoK) papers
  • Other empirical research related to the above topics

Submission Guidelines:

Papers must be submitted via EasyChair.
Submissions must not substantially overlap with previously published papers or with works that are simultaneously submitted to a journal or a conference/workshop with proceedings. Papers must be written in English, submitted as a single PDF file, and anonymized for double-blind review. Submitted papers should be at most 16 pages for long papers and 8 pages for short papers, excluding the bibliography and appendices. The total length, including the bibliography and appendices, is limited to 20 pages for long papers and 10 pages for short papers. However, reviewers are not required to read the appendices when making acceptance decisions. All papers must follow the LNCS template at the time of submission.
Accepted papers will be published in a Springer joint proceedings. At least one author of each accepted paper will be required to register for the workshop and present the work orally or as a poster.
